Variety

Variety is one of the first things a coffee plant brings to the cup.

A variety is not a flavour note. It is a form of potential. What appears later depends on place, farming, processing, roasting, brewing, and time.
Part I

What A Variety Is

Two coffee plants can grow in the same farm and still behave differently.

They may produce different fruit, different yields, different resistance to disease, and different sensory possibilities.

  1. Plant IdentityVariety begins in the genetics and behaviour of the plant.
  2. Growth HabitSome plants grow tall, some compact, some open, some dense.
  3. Fruit And SeedVariety can influence fruit development, seed size, density, and composition.
Part II

What Variety Can Influence

Variety matters because it shapes what is possible before harvest begins. Not every difference will be visible in the cup. Some will.

  1. YieldHow much fruit the plant can produce under given conditions.
  2. ResilienceHow the plant responds to disease, drought, heat, shade, and stress.
  3. RipeningHow fruit develops, matures, and concentrates sugars and acids.
  4. Sensory PotentialThe range of aroma, acidity, sweetness, body, and structure the coffee may support.
Part III

Variety Is Not Destiny

A famous variety can produce a poor coffee.

A modest variety can produce a serious coffee.

The name of the variety is not the taste of the cup.

  1. Place MattersThe same variety can behave differently in different climates and soils.
  2. Season MattersRain, heat, stress, flowering, and ripening change the crop.
  3. Processing MattersVariety gives material. Processing decides how that material is handled.
  4. Roasting MattersRoast can reveal, mute, or distort what the green coffee carries.
Part IV

Why Professionals Pay Attention

Professionals care about variety because it helps explain recurring patterns. It is one layer of diagnosis, not a final answer.

  1. ExpectationSome varieties are often associated with florals, fruit, sweetness, body, or acidity.
  2. ComparisonTasting different varieties from the same farm can reveal plant-level differences.
  3. Buying And RoastingKnowing variety can help guide selection, roast planning, and communication.
  4. Farm DecisionsVariety affects yield, risk, quality potential, and long-term planting strategy.
Part V

A Better Question

When tasting coffee, it is tempting to ask:

What variety is this?

A better professional question is:

What is this variety expressing here?

  1. HereThis soil, this climate, this farm, this season.
  2. Handled This WayThis harvest, this fermentation, this drying, this storage.
  3. Roasted This WayThis roast degree, this development, this rest.
  4. Brewed This WayThis water, grind, ratio, temperature, and method.

Page Direction

Variety provides potential.

The cup reveals what happened to that potential.
