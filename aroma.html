Coffee Experience Landscape · Page 9

Aroma

Before coffee is tasted, it is often smelled.

Many of the things people describe in coffee are not coming from the tongue. They are arriving through the nose.
Part I

The First Meeting

Aroma often reaches us before the first sip.

The cup is still on the table. The coffee has already begun.

  1. Freshly Ground CoffeeAroma released as the roasted seed is broken apart.
  2. Brewing CoffeeSteam carries volatile compounds into the air.
  3. The Cup ItselfAroma rising quietly even before drinking.
Part II

Aroma Is Movement

For something to be smelled, it must travel.

  1. Volatile CompoundsSmall molecules leave the coffee and move through the air.
  2. The Nose ReceivesReceptors respond to different patterns of molecules.
  3. The Brain InterpretsThe signal becomes recognition, memory, and meaning.
Part III

The Vocabulary Problem

Most aromas do not arrive with labels attached.

We borrow names from things we already know.

  1. FloralJasmine, rose, blossom, perfume.
  2. FruitCitrus, berry, tropical fruit, dried fruit.
  3. RoastToast, cocoa, nuts, caramel, smoke.
  4. PlantHerbal, leafy, grassy, woody, earthy.
Part IV

Aroma Changes

The same coffee does not smell exactly the same throughout the cup.

  1. HotSome aromas appear strongly when the coffee is hot.
  2. WarmOthers become clearer as temperature falls.
  3. CoolA few only become obvious near room temperature.
Part V

Aroma Beyond Coffee

A sensory vocabulary is not built inside coffee.

It is built in kitchens, markets, gardens, forests, bakeries, farms, and daily life.

Coffee simply gives us another place to notice.

  1. Smell FruitBefore eating it.
  2. Smell SpicesBefore cooking with them.
  3. Smell Leaves and FlowersNoticing builds memory.

Page Direction

Aroma is often the larger part of flavour.

When people say they taste jasmine, peach, cocoa, or smoke, the nose is usually doing much of the work.
