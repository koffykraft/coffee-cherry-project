Freshness and Aging

Coffee changes with time. Freshness and aging are not opposites. They are parts of the same journey.

The question is not whether coffee changes. The question is how it changes, how quickly it changes, and whether those changes help or harm the intended cup.
Part I

Nothing Stands Still

Coffee continues changing after harvest, after drying, after roasting, and after brewing.
  1. TimeEvery coffee experiences it.
  2. EnvironmentHeat, oxygen, moisture, and light influence the rate of change.
  3. MaterialDifferent coffees age differently.
Part II

Freshness Is Context

A coffee can be too fresh for one purpose and ideal for another.

  1. Green CoffeeFresh crop character may be desirable.
  2. Roasted CoffeeOften benefits from some rest before brewing.
  3. Brewed CoffeeChanges rapidly once prepared.
  4. PurposeFreshness must be judged in context.
Part III

What Aging Can Change

Aging affects aroma, flavour, extraction, and perception.
  1. AromaSome volatile compounds decline over time.
  2. ClarityDistinctive notes may become less visible.
  3. StructureAcidity, sweetness, body, and finish may shift.
  4. BehaviourRoasting and brewing responses may also change.
Part IV

Protecting Freshness

Freshness is not preserved by one action alone.

  1. StorageGood storage slows unwanted change.
  2. PackagingSuitable packaging helps protect quality.
  3. TemperatureHeat often accelerates deterioration.
  4. HandlingCareless handling can shorten the useful life of coffee.
Part V

A Professional View

Professionals do not ask only whether a coffee is old or fresh. They ask whether it is performing well.
  1. ObservationTaste the coffee rather than assume.
  2. ComparisonTrack how the cup changes over time.
  3. JudgementEvaluate suitability, not age alone.
  4. LearningTime teaches as much as technique.

Page Direction

Freshness is not a date. Aging is not a defect.

Both describe change. The task is to understand which changes support the cup and which diminish it.
