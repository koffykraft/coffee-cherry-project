Environment

Coffee does not grow in a general place. It grows inside a set of conditions.

Environment is not a flavour note. It is the world around the plant: temperature, rainfall, shade, wind, humidity, season, stress, and care.
Part I

More Than A Place Name

Country, region, and farm names are useful.

But they are only the beginning. A plant does not respond to a name. It responds to conditions.

  1. TemperatureHeat, coolness, and the difference between day and night.
  2. RainfallHow much rain falls, when it falls, and how dry periods arrive.
  3. HumidityMoisture in the air, affecting plant health, disease pressure, and drying.
  4. LightSun, shade, cloud cover, canopy, and exposure.
Part II

The Plant Responds

Coffee is a living plant. It adjusts continuously to its surroundings.

  1. GrowthEnvironment influences leaf, branch, root, flower, and fruit development.
  2. FloweringRain, dry periods, and seasonal rhythm affect when flowering begins.
  3. Fruit DevelopmentThe cherry develops within the conditions of that season.
  4. StressDrought, heat, disease, poor nutrition, and excess exposure can change plant behaviour.
Part III

Altitude Is Not Flavour

Altitude is often mentioned because it is easy to measure.

But altitude does not create flavour by itself. It changes temperature, ripening speed, weather exposure, and farming conditions.

  1. Cooler ConditionsHigher elevations often bring lower average temperatures.
  2. Slower RipeningIn cooler conditions, fruit development may take longer.
  3. Different RisksWind, rain, access, labour, disease pressure, and harvest timing also matter.
Part IV

Shade And Sun

Coffee can be grown under full sun, partial shade, or heavier shade. None is automatically best. Each creates trade-offs.

  1. Full SunCan increase production, but may increase stress and management needs.
  2. Partial ShadeCan moderate heat and light while allowing productive growth.
  3. Heavy ShadeCan protect the plant, but may slow growth or reduce yield.
  4. Canopy LifeShade also changes humidity, soil cover, insects, birds, and farm ecology.
Part V

What Reaches The Cup

Many environmental effects never become obvious to the drinker.

Some do. A professional learns to ask what conditions produced the material before judging the cup alone.

  1. RipenessEnvironment affects how evenly and fully fruit matures.
  2. DensityGrowing conditions can influence seed structure and roasting behaviour.
  3. BalanceSweetness, acidity, body, and aroma are shaped by many conditions together.
  4. SeasonThe same farm can produce different coffees in different years.

Page Direction

Environment does not determine flavour by itself.

It shapes the conditions under which flavour potential develops.
