How Do We Recognize Things?

Recognition is not magic. The body receives signals. The mind searches for a match.

Nobody begins with a complete flavour vocabulary. We learn by meeting things, remembering them, and meeting them again.
Part I

A Signal Arrives

Coffee gives signals. Aroma molecules reach the nose. Taste compounds reach the tongue. Heat and texture reach the mouth.

The body does not receive a word. It receives signals.

  1. NoseDetects volatile aroma compounds rising from the cup and returning during the sip.
  2. TongueDetects broad taste signals such as sweet, sour, bitter, salty, and umami.
  3. MouthFeels heat, weight, thickness, smoothness, dryness, and astringency.
Part II

The Mind Searches

The brain compares the signal with what it already knows. Recognition is often a search through memory.

  1. Known ThingsOrange, jaggery, smoke, tea, pepper, cocoa, mango, medicine, wet soil.
  2. Near MatchesSometimes the mind finds something close, not exact.
  3. No Match YetSometimes we notice something but cannot name it. That is still noticing.
Part III

People Differ

Two people can drink the same coffee and say different things.

They may have different memories, habits, foods, languages, sensitivities, or expectations.

  1. MemoryA descriptor is easier when the person has met that aroma before.
  2. CultureOne person may say jaggery where another says caramel.
  3. SensitivitySome people notice bitterness, acidity, florals, smoke, or ferment more strongly than others.
  4. ContextA label, a story, a price, or a beautiful cup can change attention.
Part IV

Words Come Late

A descriptor is not the experience. It is a word placed after the experience.

  1. First NoticeIs it sharp, soft, heavy, sweet, dry, clean, warm, smoky, fresh?
  2. Then CompareWhat does it remind you of?
  3. Then NameA word can help, but it should not arrive too early.
Part V

Learning Is Repeated Attention

Recognition improves slowly. Not through pressure. Through return.

Smell the fruit before eating it. Notice spices while cooking. Taste coffee as it cools. Compare one cup with another.

  1. Build MemorySmell and taste ordinary things with more attention.
  2. Compare GentlyOne cup beside another teaches faster than one cup alone.
  3. Accept Uncertainty“I notice something, but I cannot name it yet” is a valid observation.

Page Direction

Recognition grows from memory.

The aim is not to guess the correct tasting note. The aim is to become more available to what is already present.
