Flavor

Flavor is what happens when several senses meet.

Flavor is bigger than taste. It is not only the tongue. It is aroma, taste, mouthfeel, temperature, memory, and attention arriving together.
Part I

Not One Thing

A cup of coffee does not send one message.

It sends many small messages at the same time. The mind gathers them and calls the result flavour.

  1. AromaWhat the nose receives before, during, and after the sip.
  2. TasteSweet, sour, bitter, salty, umami.
  3. FeelingHeat, weight, smoothness, dryness, thickness, sharpness.
Part II

Why Coffee Seems Complex

Coffee often feels complex because several signals overlap. One part may suggest fruit. Another may suggest bitterness. Another may feel creamy or dry.

  1. One Cup, Many LayersSweetness, acidity, aroma, body, and aftertaste can all appear together.
  2. One Word, Many Sources“Chocolate” may involve bitterness, roast aroma, sweetness, and texture.
  3. One Person, Many MemoriesRecognition depends on what the drinker already knows.
Part III

Descriptor Words Are Shortcuts

When someone says peach, they may not mean the coffee contains peach.

They may mean the cup reminds them of peach: soft fruit aroma, gentle sweetness, light acidity, perhaps a certain roundness.

  1. ChocolateMay point to cocoa-like bitterness, roast aroma, and sweetness.
  2. CitrusMay point to acidity, brightness, peel aroma, or freshness.
  3. FloralMay point to perfume-like aroma rather than taste on the tongue.
  4. WineyMay point to fruit, acidity, fermentation, aroma, and body together.
Part IV

Flavor Changes in the Cup

A coffee may not reveal everything at once. Heat, air, and time shift the experience.

  1. First SipOften shows heat, intensity, bitterness, and general impression.
  2. Middle of the CupBalance becomes easier to read.
  3. Cooling CupSweetness, acidity, fruit, and defects may become clearer.
Part V

How to Read Flavor

Do not begin by asking, “What note is this?”

Begin smaller.

  1. Is It Light or Heavy?Start with structure.
  2. Is It Bright or Soft?Notice acidity and shape.
  3. Is It Sweet, Bitter, Dry, Clean?Find the broad signals first.
  4. What Does It Remind You Of?Only then move toward descriptors.

Page Direction

Flavor is a meeting place.

Aroma, taste, feeling, memory, and temperature all contribute. That is why flavour can be simple to enjoy and difficult to explain.
