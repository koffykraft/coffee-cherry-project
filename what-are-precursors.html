What Are Precursors?

Before flavour appears, coffee carries material that can become flavour.

A precursor is not a flavour note. It is part of the material that may later participate in aroma, taste, colour, body, or roast character.
Part I

Before Flavour Appears

Green coffee does not yet smell or taste like roasted coffee.

Most of the familiar aromas of roasted coffee appear later, through transformation. But the material for that transformation must already be present.

  1. Green CoffeeThe seed before roasting, carrying stored material and structure.
  2. PotentialMaterial that may later become aroma, taste, colour, or body.
  3. TransformationHeat, time, microbes, moisture, and water decide what changes.
Part II

What A Precursor Is

A precursor is material that can take part in a later reaction or change. It is not the finished experience.

  1. SugarsImportant in browning reactions and sweetness-related aroma development.
  2. Amino AcidsImportant partners in roast reactions that form many aroma compounds.
  3. Organic AcidsPart of acidity, balance, and transformation during roasting.
  4. LipidsPart of coffee structure, mouthfeel, aroma retention, and ageing behaviour.
  5. Phenolic CompoundsLinked with bitterness, astringency, antioxidant chemistry, and roast change.
Part III

Where Precursors Come From

Precursors do not arrive at the roaster by accident.

They are shaped before roasting, through the life of the plant and the handling of the fruit and seed.

  1. VarietyThe genetic starting point for what the plant can build.
  2. EnvironmentTemperature, rainfall, shade, stress, and season influence development.
  3. Soil And NutritionRoot health, minerals, water, and soil life affect plant performance.
  4. ProcessingFruit contact, fermentation, washing, and drying shape what reaches storage.
  5. Storage And RestTime and conditions can preserve, reduce, or damage the available material.
Part IV

What Happens To Precursors

During roasting, precursor material does not simply remain as it was. Some of it transforms. Some is consumed. Some contributes indirectly. Some never becomes noticeable in the cup.

  1. FormationNew aroma and flavour-active compounds can form during roasting.
  2. LossSome compounds break down, evaporate, or become less important.
  3. BalanceThe final cup depends on many transformations happening together.
  4. LimitsRoasting cannot create quality from material that is not there.
Part V

Why Professionals Care

Roasters do not create flavour from nothing.

They work with the material the coffee already carries. A careful roast can reveal potential. It can also hide it, flatten it, or push it too far.

  1. Reading Green CoffeeDensity, moisture, process, age, and appearance give clues, not certainty.
  2. Roast PlanningDifferent coffees respond differently to heat.
  3. Cup EvaluationThe cup shows which possibilities became visible.
  4. HumilityGood roasting respects the material instead of forcing a fixed result.
