What Are Precursors?

Transformation · Page 27

Coffee carries potential. Heat, time, microbes, moisture, and water decide which parts of that potential become visible.

Part I · Before Flavour Appears

A roasted coffee contains hundreds of aroma compounds. Most of them did not exist in the same form inside the fresh seed.

Something existed before them.

Those earlier materials are often called precursors.

  1. Sugars.
  2. Amino acids.
  3. Organic acids.
  4. Lipids.
  5. Phenolic compounds.

Part II · Potential, Not Promise

A precursor is not a flavour.

It is material that may later participate in creating flavour.

The presence of a precursor does not guarantee a particular cup outcome.

  1. Potential exists.
  2. Transformation follows.
  3. Results vary.

Part III · Where Precursors Come From

Precursors begin forming long before roasting.

  1. The variety.
  2. The environment.
  3. The soil.
  4. The season.
  5. The fruit.
  6. The processing.

Everything encountered in the previous pages contributes to the material that reaches the roaster.

Part IV · Why Professionals Care

Roasters do not create flavour from nothing.

They work with material already present in the coffee.

The richer and more suitable the precursor pool, the more possibilities may become available during roasting.

  1. Some coffees transform easily.
  2. Some resist transformation.
  3. Some reveal complexity.
  4. Some remain simple.

Part V · A Useful Way To Think

Instead of asking:

“What flavours are inside this green coffee?”

Ask:

“What flavour possibilities does this coffee carry?”

That question is usually closer to reality.
