Development

Development is the continued shaping of coffee during roasting.

Development is not simply “more roast”.

It is the period where the roaster continues guiding sweetness, acidity, aroma, body, bitterness, and roast character toward a chosen balance.
Part I

A Word With Weight

In roasting, development is often used in a narrow technical way.

In this landscape, we use it more simply: how the coffee continues changing as heat does its work.

  1. TimeHow long change is allowed to continue.
  2. HeatHow strongly energy continues entering the coffee.
  3. DirectionWhich aspects of the coffee are being emphasised or reduced.
Part II

What Moves During Development

As roasting continues, the cup does not change in one direction only. Several things move at once.

  1. AcidityMay become softer, rounder, or less visible.
  2. SweetnessMay become clearer, darker, heavier, or eventually muted.
  3. AromaCan move from floral and fruit toward caramel, nut, chocolate, spice, or roast.
  4. BodyMay feel fuller, rounder, thinner, or heavier depending on roast choices.
  5. BitternessCan become structured, pleasant, dominant, or harsh.
Part III

Underdeveloped And Overdeveloped

These words are useful only when used carefully.

They should describe what is happening in the cup, not simply whether a roast is light or dark.

  1. UnderdevelopedMay taste grassy, raw, sharp, thin, sour, or unfinished.
  2. Well DevelopedMay show clarity, sweetness, structure, and appropriate roast character.
  3. OverdevelopedMay taste flat, baked, heavy, burnt, dull, or dominated by roast.
  4. ContextA roast is judged by purpose, coffee material, brew method, and cup quality.
Part IV

Development Is Trade-Off

A roaster cannot maximise everything at once. A choice that helps one part of the cup may reduce another.

  1. More BrightnessMay preserve acidity and aromatics, but risk sharpness or thinness.
  2. More SweetnessMay require enough transformation for balance and roundness.
  3. More Roast CharacterMay add chocolate, nut, caramel, or smoke, but reduce origin clarity.
  4. More SolubilityMay make brewing easier, but can come with heavier roast influence.
Part V

The Cup Guides The Word

Development should not be judged from roast colour alone.

The cup must be tasted. A light roast can be well developed. A dark roast can still be poorly developed.

  1. Is It Finished?Does the coffee taste complete, or raw and unresolved?
  2. Is It Clear?Are the coffee’s useful qualities readable?
  3. Is It Balanced?Do acidity, sweetness, bitterness, aroma, body, and finish work together?
  4. Is It Suitable?Does the development serve the intended brew and drinker?

Page Direction

Development is not a number by itself.

It is the way roasting continues shaping the coffee until the cup reaches a useful expression.
