Processing

Processing begins after harvest. It decides how fruit, seed, water, microbes, and time are allowed to interact.

Processing is often described by names: washed, natural, honey, pulped natural. The names are useful. But the work itself is more basic: remove, retain, ferment, wash, dry, and protect.
Part I

After Picking

Once coffee is picked, the cherry is no longer part of the living plant.

But it is not finished. Fruit, mucilage, seed, moisture, microbes, and heat continue to change.

  1. SortingRemoving unripe, overripe, damaged, floating, or unwanted fruit.
  2. DecisionChoosing how much fruit material stays with the seed.
  3. ControlManaging time, temperature, water, oxygen, and cleanliness.
Part II

The Main Question

Most processing methods can be understood through one question: how much of the fruit remains with the seed, and for how long?

  1. Removed EarlyFruit skin and pulp are removed soon after harvest.
  2. Kept LongerThe seed dries with more fruit material around it.
  3. Partly RemovedSome mucilage remains during drying.
  4. Guided DifferentlyModern methods may control fermentation, oxygen, temperature, or microbes more deliberately.
Part III

Common Processing Families

These names are starting points, not complete explanations.

Two coffees with the same processing name may still taste very different.

  1. WashedFruit is removed early. The seed is usually washed before drying.
  2. NaturalThe whole cherry dries with fruit material around the seed.
  3. HoneySkin is removed, but some mucilage remains during drying.
  4. Pulped NaturalA related approach where pulp is removed and mucilage remains during drying.
Part IV

What Processing Can Influence

Processing does not create quality by itself. It guides material that already exists. Good fruit still matters. Careful drying still matters.

  1. ClarityHow cleanly acidity, sweetness, and aroma appear.
  2. Fruit CharacterHow much ripe, dried, fermented, or jam-like fruit appears.
  3. BodyHow heavy, round, thin, or textured the cup feels.
  4. RiskPoor control can create mould, rot, harsh fermentation, or unstable drying.
Part V

Do Not Stop At The Name

A label may say washed or natural. That is not enough.

A professional asks what actually happened: the fruit quality, the time, the water, the fermentation, the drying, and the care.

  1. What Was Picked?Processing cannot fully repair poor harvest selection.
  2. What Was Removed?Skin, pulp, mucilage, water, defects, or nothing at first.
  3. What Was Allowed?Fermentation, contact time, oxygen, heat, and microbial activity.
  4. How Was It Dried?Drying often decides whether processing succeeds.

Page Direction

Processing is guided change.

The name of the method is useful only after we understand what changed, what was controlled, and what was allowed to remain.
