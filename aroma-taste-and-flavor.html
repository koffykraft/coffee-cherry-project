Aroma, Taste and Flavor

Three words sit close together. They are not the same.

A person may say a coffee tastes like jasmine, orange, chocolate, or smoke. Most of those words are not tastes. They are usually aroma, memory, and flavour working together.
Part I

Taste Is Small

Taste is what the tongue can directly sense.

It matters. It is not the whole cup.

  1. SweetSugar-like, rounded, softening.
  2. SourAcidic, sharp, bright, sometimes fruit-like.
  3. BitterCoffee-like, cocoa-like, harsh, pleasant, or drying depending on balance.
  4. Salty and UmamiUsually quieter in coffee, but still part of taste language.
Part II

Aroma Is Large

Aroma does much of the work people casually call taste. It reaches us before drinking, and again while drinking.

  1. Before the SipSteam carries aroma from the cup to the nose.
  2. During the SipAroma moves from the mouth back into the nose.
  3. After the SipSome aromas linger as aftertaste.
Part III

Flavor Is the Combination

Flavor is not one signal.

It is the cup assembled by the body: aroma, taste, heat, texture, memory, and attention.

  1. AromaFloral, fruity, nutty, spicy, smoky, herbal, roasted.
  2. TasteSweet, sour, bitter, salty, umami.
  3. FeelingThin, heavy, creamy, dry, smooth, sharp, warm.
  4. MemoryThe mind reaches for things it already knows.
Part IV

Why Labels Can Confuse

A bag may say blueberry. The cup may not give blueberry to you. That does not mean you failed.

  1. Labels Are HintsThey point toward a possible experience. They do not command one.
  2. Memory DiffersOne person’s berry may be another person’s jam, wine, or sweetness.
  3. Conditions ChangeGrind, water, temperature, age, and brew method can shift what appears.
  4. Attention LearnsDescriptors become easier only after repeated noticing.
Part V

A Small Test

Take a sip while gently pinching your nose. Then release.

The difference is aroma returning.

  1. Nose ClosedThe cup becomes flatter. Taste and texture remain.
  2. Nose OpenAroma returns, and the coffee feels more complete.

Page Direction

Do not rush to name the flavour.

First ask what is happening: smell, taste, texture, temperature, memory, or all together.
