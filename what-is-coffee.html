What Is Coffee?

Most people meet coffee in a cup. Perhaps black. Perhaps with milk. Perhaps as part of a morning routine so familiar that it hardly seems worth questioning. Yet the cup is the end of a long journey, not the beginning.

Coffee begins as a living plant. That plant produces flowers. The flowers become fruit. Inside the fruit are seeds. Those seeds are dried, roasted, ground, and eventually brewed. Everything we know as coffee emerges from that sequence.

Coffee is not a beverage that comes from a bean. Coffee is a seed that comes from a fruit.

The Plant

Coffee belongs to the genus Coffea. It grows throughout the tropical world and has been cultivated for centuries. Like any other plant, it responds to sunlight, rainfall, temperature, soil, altitude, insects, disease, and age.

Long before coffee becomes a beverage, it exists as roots in the soil, leaves in the canopy, flowers after rain, and fruit developing on a branch.

The Fruit

The fruit of the coffee plant is commonly called a coffee cherry. It begins green and slowly ripens over many months. Depending on species and variety, it may become red, yellow, orange, purple, or nearly black.

Inside the fruit are usually two seeds. Most of the world's coffee industry is built around those seeds.

The Seed

The coffee bean is not technically a bean. It is a seed. Its natural purpose is simple: to produce another coffee plant.

Roasting changes that purpose completely. Heat transforms the seed into something aromatic, soluble, and capable of producing one of the world's most recognisable beverages.

The Beverage

Brewing extracts compounds from roasted coffee into water. Some contribute aroma. Some contribute taste. Some contribute body, bitterness, sweetness, acidity, or aftertaste.

The resulting beverage can vary enormously from one coffee to another, yet every cup traces its origins back to the same plant and the same fruit.

Coffee Within Buna

Within the Buna Library, coffee is approached as a landscape rather than a product. The plant matters. The fruit matters. The seed matters. The leaf matters. The transformations matter.

The cup remains important, but it is only one expression of a much larger story.
