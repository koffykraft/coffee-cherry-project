Roasting

Roasting is not the creation of coffee. It is the transformation of coffee.

The roaster does not begin with a blank canvas. The variety, environment, fruit development, processing, drying, storage, and precursor pool have already done much of the work.
Part I

What Roasting Does

Roasting applies heat to green coffee and changes its chemistry, structure, colour, aroma, and solubility.

The seed becomes the material we recognise as roasted coffee.

  1. 1
    HeatEnergy enters the coffee and drives change.
  2. 2
    TransformationExisting compounds react, break apart, combine, and evolve.
  3. 3
    PreparationThe coffee becomes suitable for grinding and brewing.
Part II

Roasting Is A Series Of Changes

Roasting is often described by temperatures and times. The coffee experiences a sequence of physical and chemical events.

  1. 4
    Drying PhaseWater is reduced and the seed warms internally.
  2. 5
    BrowningMany colour and aroma-forming reactions accelerate.
  3. 6
    ExpansionThe structure changes as gases form and pressure increases.
  4. 7
    DevelopmentThe balance of aroma, acidity, sweetness, bitterness, and body continues to shift.
Part III

Revealing And Removing

Roasting reveals some possibilities and removes others.

Every roast is a set of choices and trade-offs.

  1. 8
    FormationNew aroma compounds appear.
  2. 9
    ReductionSome acids and precursor materials decline.
  3. 10
    BalanceDifferent roast approaches emphasise different aspects of the coffee.
  4. 11
    LimitsNot every desirable quality can be maximised at the same time.
Part IV

What Roasters Actually Manage

Roasters speak about curves, rates, airflow, charge temperatures, and development. Behind those terms is control of energy and change.

  1. 12
    TimeHow quickly or slowly changes occur.
  2. 13
    TemperatureThe level and application of heat.
  3. 14
    AirflowMovement of heat, smoke, and moisture.
  4. 15
    ConsistencyRepeating desired outcomes reliably.
Part V

The Cup Is The Test

Roast data can guide decisions.

The cup remains the final judge.

  1. 16
    ExpressionHas the coffee expressed its potential clearly?
  2. 17
    BalanceHow do aroma, taste, body, and finish relate?
  3. 18
    SuitabilityIs the roast appropriate for its purpose?
  4. 19
    LearningEvery roast becomes information for the next one.

Page Direction

Roasting is guided transformation.

It does not begin the story of the coffee. It changes the story that already exists.
